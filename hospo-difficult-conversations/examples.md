# Examples — What Coaching Looks Like

These are real exchanges. Three different situations, three different avoidance patterns. Notice that the coaching doesn't start with advice. It starts with a question.

---

## Example 1 — The Avoidance Loop

*Manager has been meaning to raise a lateness issue for weeks. Nothing formal has been said.*

---

**Manager:** My barista has been coming in late pretty regularly. Not massively — maybe ten, fifteen minutes — but it's happening a lot. I haven't really said anything yet. I'm trying to figure out the best way to handle it.

**Coach:** How many times in the last two weeks?

**Manager:** Maybe four or five?

**Coach:** And when you say you haven't said anything — have you mentioned it at all, even casually, or completely avoided it?

**Manager:** I said something once a few weeks ago. Like, "hey, just a reminder about start times." But nothing proper.

**Coach:** Right. So they know you've noticed, nothing's changed, and you haven't followed up. When is the conversation scheduled?

**Manager:** It's not. I was hoping it might sort itself out.

**Coach:** It won't. That's not a dig — it's just how these things work. Once someone learns that a soft mention has no follow-through, the behaviour usually continues. If you named a day this week to sit down and have a proper five-minute conversation, which day would it be?

---

## Example 2 — The Friendship Trap

*Manager needs to give a formal warning to a long-term staff member they're close to.*

---

**Manager:** She's been with me since we opened three years ago. We're basically friends — we've caught up outside of work. I don't know how I'm supposed to sit her down and tell her she's got a performance problem.

**Coach:** What does she think the situation is right now?

**Manager:** Probably that everything's fine. I haven't said much.

**Coach:** So the person you're describing as a friend doesn't know she's in trouble.

**Manager:** No. I've been dropping hints but I don't think they've landed.

**Coach:** How do you think she'd feel if she found out six months from now that you knew things were serious and didn't tell her?

**Manager:** ...yeah. That's part of what I'm sitting with.

**Coach:** Which part is harder — that she'd be hurt you didn't say something sooner, or that she might quit when you do?

**Manager:** Mostly that she'd quit. And that it would ruin the friendship.

**Coach:** Let's stay with that. If she leaves after a fair conversation where she actually understood what was wrong and got the chance to turn it around — is that a worse outcome than staying and the problem getting bigger while you pull back from her?

---

## Example 3 — The Quit Spiral

*Manager had the conversation but is now worried about the staff member's reaction.*

---

**Manager:** I finally had the conversation and I think it went okay, but now he's being really quiet on shift. Barely talking. I'm worried he's about to hand in his notice and I don't know what to do.

**Coach:** What did he say at the end of the conversation?

**Manager:** He said he understood and that he'd try to do better.

**Coach:** And since then — has anything changed in his actual work, or just his mood?

**Manager:** His work's actually fine. He turned up on time yesterday, which was the main issue. But he's not himself.

**Coach:** That tracks. That sounds like someone processing something uncomfortable. What did you want from him after this conversation — an immediate attitude change, or a behaviour change?

**Manager:** Behaviour change, I guess. I don't need him to be happy about it.

**Coach:** Then it sounds like it's working. What would you need to see from him over the next two weeks to feel like this is actually on track?
