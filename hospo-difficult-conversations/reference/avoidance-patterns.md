# Avoidance Patterns — Reference

This file is for the coach. It describes how avoidance shows up in hospo management — what it sounds like, why it works in the short term, and what to listen for.

---

## The five avoidance patterns

### 1. Passive waiting
*"I was hoping it would sort itself out."*

The manager believes the problem is temporary or self-correcting. They're watching without acting, treating silence as a kind of management. This pattern is usually the earliest stage. It resolves when the problem gets worse or the manager reaches a breaking point.

Listen for: vague timelines ("recently," "for a while"), no specific incidents, the word "hoping."

### 2. Perpetual delay
*"It's not the right time."*

The manager has decided to act but keeps finding reasons to defer. Busy service. Someone's having a rough week outside work. The run-up to a long weekend. There will always be a reason.

Listen for: acknowledgment that something needs to be said, followed by conditions. "Once things settle down." "When the new roster's sorted." "After the weekend."

### 3. The soft mention
*"I've mentioned it."*

The manager has said something — once, casually, during service — and is counting that as a completed conversation. From the staff member's perspective, it may not have registered as anything significant.

Listen for: the word "mentioned." Ask what was said, when, in what context, and what the staff member's response was. A soft mention and a performance conversation are different things.

### 4. Minimising
*"It's not that bad."*

The manager is tracking the problem but rationalising its size downward. This is often paired with a comparison: "It's not as bad as it was." "At least they're not doing [worse thing]."

Listen for: relativism. The question is not whether it's the worst thing possible — it's whether it's affecting the team or the business.

### 5. Catastrophising the outcome
*"If I say something, she'll quit."*

The manager has run a mental simulation of the conversation and decided it ends badly — staff member walks out, team takes sides, atmosphere sours for months. This simulation is speculative. The manager is protecting themselves from an imagined version of the conversation.

Listen for: certainty about a future that hasn't happened. *How do you know that?* is often the right response. Then: *And if she does quit after a fair conversation where she got the chance to fix it — what does that tell you?*

## What avoidance costs (use these when needed)

- The team is watching. They're drawing conclusions about what the manager tolerates.
- The problem compounds. Lateness normalises. Attitude spreads. The manager's own resentment builds.
- The staff member loses the chance to understand the problem and correct it.
- The longer it runs, the harder the conversation becomes — and the more likely it becomes formal.
