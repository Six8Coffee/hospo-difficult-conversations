# Conversation Framework — Reference

This file is for the coach. Load it once the manager has committed to a time and is preparing for the conversation itself.

---

## The structure

A performance conversation in hospo has six parts. The whole thing can take five minutes if it's a first conversation about a contained issue. More if it's been building.

### 1. Open with intent, not apology

Don't start with "I've been meaning to bring this up" or "I'm sorry to have to say this." That signals discomfort and invites the staff member to manage your feelings before you've said anything.

Start with: *"I wanted to have a proper chat about something specific. Thanks for making the time."*

### 2. State the observation — not the judgment

Describe behaviour, not character. Specific, factual, recent.

Not: *"You've been really unreliable lately."*  
Instead: *"You've called out on Saturday three times in the last six weeks."*

Not: *"Your attitude has been off."*  
Instead: *"In last Tuesday's briefing, when I asked about the new workflow, you said 'whatever' and walked off. I want to talk about that."*

The observation must be specific enough that the staff member can't reasonably dispute the facts, only the interpretation.

### 3. State the impact

Connect the behaviour to the team or the business. Not a lecture — one or two sentences.

*"When you call out on Saturday, I'm short-staffed on our busiest day. The rest of the team picks it up. That's not sustainable."*

### 4. Ask for their perspective

Stop talking. This is the part most managers skip.

*"I want to understand what's going on from your side."*

Then actually listen. There may be something you don't know. There may not be. Either way, the staff member needs to feel heard before they can hear what comes next.

### 5. Agree on what changes

Be specific. Not "I need you to do better." Name what changes and by when.

*"What I need from the next four weeks is attendance on the shifts you're rostered. If something comes up, I need to know before the day — not the morning of. Can we agree on that?"*

### 6. Set the next check-in

Don't end the conversation without a date to review.

*"Let's check back in on this in three weeks. I'll put some time in the diary."*

This signals that you're tracking it. It also gives the staff member a clear window to show improvement.

---

## What to do when the conversation gets hard

**If they get upset or emotional:** Let them. Don't immediately walk it back or soften what you've said. *"Take a moment. I'm not going anywhere."* Then continue when they're ready.

**If they get defensive or deny the facts:** Don't argue. Return to the specific behaviour. *"I'm not questioning your effort overall. I'm talking about these three specific Saturdays."*

**If they raise a counter-grievance:** Acknowledge it without pivoting. *"I want to hear that. Let's make time to talk about it separately. Right now I need to finish what I started."*

**If they threaten to quit:** Don't panic and walk it back. *"I hope it doesn't come to that. But I also can't ignore this issue — that wouldn't be fair to the rest of the team."*

---

## After the conversation

Write a brief note to yourself that day: date, who was present, what was discussed, what was agreed. Keep it somewhere safe. If this becomes a formal process, that record matters.

This is called documenting. You don't need a form — a timestamped email to yourself is enough.
