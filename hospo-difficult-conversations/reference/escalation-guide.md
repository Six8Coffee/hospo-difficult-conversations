# Escalation Guide — Reference

This file is for the coach. Load it when the situation has moved beyond informal performance coaching into formal disciplinary territory, or when the manager is asking about warnings, termination, or legal obligations.

**Note to coach:** For anything at Verbal Warning level or above, advise the manager to call Fair Work Australia before proceeding — 13 13 94. This guide provides orientation, not legal advice.

---

## The escalation ladder

### Stage 1 — Informal discussion
What this coach handles. No formal record required, though it's good practice to note it.

Used for: first conversations about attendance, performance, or attitude. Issues that are addressable without formal process.

### Stage 2 — Verbal warning
Formal. Must be documented. The staff member should be told explicitly that this is a verbal warning.

Used for: the issue has been raised informally and hasn't improved, or the initial incident is serious enough to warrant a formal response.

What to do:
- Inform the staff member that this is a formal verbal warning
- State the specific behaviour and the expected change
- Document it: date, staff member name, what was said, what was agreed, both parties' signatures if possible
- Give the staff member a copy

### Stage 3 — Written warning
Formal letter. Must be signed and filed.

Used for: previous verbal warning(s) with no improvement, or a more serious incident.

A written warning typically sets out:
- The performance or conduct issue
- Previous discussions and warnings
- What improvement is required and by when
- Consequences if improvement doesn't occur

### Stage 4 — Show Cause
Used for serious misconduct or repeated failure to improve after warnings.

The staff member is asked to show cause as to why their employment should not be terminated. They should be given reasonable time to respond (usually in writing).

**Call Fair Work before issuing show cause.**

### Stage 5 — Termination
Requires that a fair process has been followed — adequate warning, opportunity to respond, documented record.

Summary dismissal (termination without notice) is only appropriate for serious misconduct: theft, violence, fraud, gross insubordination. For performance issues, you need the escalation process.

**Call Fair Work before terminating.** Unfair dismissal claims are real and costly.

---

## What "documenting" means in practice

You don't need an HR system. A timestamped email to yourself after every significant conversation is enough. Record:
- Date and time
- Who was present
- What was discussed (brief summary)
- What was agreed or decided

Keep these somewhere you can retrieve them. If a matter goes to Fair Work, this record is what demonstrates that a fair process was followed.

## Fair Work Australia
**Phone:** 13 13 94  
**Web:** fairwork.gov.au  

Use the Fair Work InfoLine for questions about notice periods, unfair dismissal timeframes, entitlements, and what a defensible process looks like for your situation. It's free and confidential.

Hospo-specific consideration: Award rates, overtime, and penalty rates are frequently misapplied in cafés and restaurants. If a conduct matter opens up questions about pay, get clarity before the conversation goes further.
